KJ Martin Is Eligible For An Extension; Should We Give It To Him, And At What Price?

One of the biggest matters for the Houston Rockets to address as the off-season begins is whether or not to extend Kenyon Martin Jr, and, if so, at what price. At the end of the day, there’s always a player who needs to get paid. Last offseason, that player was Jae’Sean Tate, who signed a 3 year/$22.1 million extension on June 30th, 2022. There has already been indications that this deal may be referenced as a marker for what someone like Martin may get paid. The thing is, though, it’s not a guarantee that it will even happen at all. Below are some reasons for and against this idea as well as my opinions on the matter.

Positives

KJ Martin has been stellar this season. He’s currently averaging 12.7 points, 5.5 rebounds, 1.3 assists, 0.5 blocks, and 0.5 steals per game in about 28 minutes a night. His efficiency sits at 57% from the field but just 31.8% from 3. Keep in mind that he’s also playing in undesirable circumstances with Houston, and wasn't even a starter until Eric Gordon was moved at the deadline. That transaction proved to be a good idea, as now the starting lineup is performing even better with Martin instead of Gordon. The difference becomes even bigger with Tari Eason in the lineup instead of Jabari Smith Jr., but that’s besides the point.

Giving him somewhere around the 8 to 10 million dollar range per year also makes Martin the perfect tradable contract when the time comes. Right now, several other teams are interested in acquiring his skill set, yet are struggling to offer a reasonable package of assets in return while making the trade actually possible. If his salary was more than just approximately $2 million a season, then more players could be offered in return, due to the way trades work in the NBA. It would be easier to make the money match. Another point is that we may get more team-friendly terms on the contract, in exchange for giving him more money upfront next season. If we wait for him to play another year on essentially a minimum deal, then the following summer he’s going to want a lot bigger payday.

Negatives

It probably goes without saying, but the biggest negative here is money. The salary cap exists for a reason, and it may not be worth it to spend a decent portion of that space on Kenyon Martin Jr. He’s good, don’t get me wrong, I just think there could be better options. We’re going to have upwards of $60 million dollars in cap space this summer, and a plethora of free agents to choose from. We must spend wisely. Additionally, we’re forfeiting the opportunity to have a solid bench player on the roster for less than $2 million next season. That’s something that shouldn’t be taken lightly. Just look at Eric Gordon, who I mentioned earlier and plays a similar role with the Clippers; he’s making close to $20 million, or more than 10 times the amount.

If our goal is to win as many games as possible next season, giving him that much money just isn’t the best option. Martin already asked out of Houston before the season started. He likely won’t be content for the next 3-4 years unless we either overpay him, or promise him a starting role moving forward. I’m not the biggest fan of either option. His shooting isn’t good enough to take up a wing spot in the starting lineup, and I’d much prefer a score-first type player as the 6th man. Another point to factor in is that if Alperen Sengun gets traded this off-season, as many rumors suggest, then Martin’s value to this team drops even more. He would be decent without Sengun, but we would likely see at least some drop off in production.

My Stance

I would say don’t let Martin be a priority, but don’t rule it out either. This decision needs to be made once we know more details about the roster moving forward. Currently, I’m leaning towards extending him as a trade piece to move at the 2024 trade deadline or off-season. By doing this, we get more time to see how he’s going to develop, and give ourselves the option to make a “win-now” move if the team starts to perform better than expected. Losing him for nothing is a risk to take that probably should be avoided. There’s clearly some value there to extract. Regardless, this decision isn’t up to me, so it’ll be interesting to see what happens. Luckily, the NBA season is almost over. Less than 1 week left before the long-awaited off-season can commence.
