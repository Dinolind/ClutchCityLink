What Would Trading Jalen Green Actually Look Like This Early In His Career

Recently, news dropped about the Rockets discussing the possibility of potentially trading Jalen Green for a star player. While these rumors were quickly shot down, it brought up an interesting topic about what would happen in such a scenario. As is, Green is still extremely unproven but has the potential to be something special. Since entering the league in 2021, we’ve yet to see him contribute to any real team success. As a result, many have begun to question what his value would be on the trade market. We rarely see teams deal their recent high draft pick before their 3rd year. Should we even be considering a Green trade, or is it something that simply won’t happen regardless of the circumstances?

Green’s Trade Value?

Like I mentioned before, we usually don’t see teams trading high-drafted young talent. When we do, it’s usually because that player has significantly unperformed in their first few seasons. A few examples I’ll point to are Markelle Fultz, James Wiseman, Lonzo Ball, and Marvin Bagley III. By that point, the return is usually very underwhelming when you remember that player was once a top-3 pick. Jalen Green is nowhere near that low in value comparatively, but it shows what could happen if his career takes a turn for the worse. However, he could also emerge as one of the top players in the league as soon as a couple of seasons from now. What the Rockets have to decide is if they want to be the team to take that risk.

I think the closest comp we do have is when Brandon Ingram was traded from the Pelicans for Anthony Davis. Davis was traded for Ingram, Lonzo Ball, Josh Hart, 3 firsts, and 1 swap. If we equate that kind of package to our roster, it would be something like Green, Tari Eason, Jae’Sean Tate, 2 firsts, and 2 swaps. That’s the kind of value I think Green has. He’s enough to be the lead piece in a trade for a star, but not enough to get it done on his own. Tari Eason may not have as much upside as Ball did at the time, but I would say his value is reasonably equivalent. Regardless, with that collection of picks and players, we could go get someone in the second tier of NBA stars. I think anyone outside of Curry, Giannis, Embiid, Jokic, Lebron, Tatum, Durant, and Luka would be potentially on the table in this hypothetical scenario.

Further Exploring Possibilities

Say we did make a trade like the one described above; where does that leave us as a franchise? A move of that caliber would likely come along with signing James Harden this summer. Then we would have a starting lineup of Harden, star-level SG from trade (Paul George for example), wing from free agency, Jabari, and Sengun. That team makes it no further than the second round in my opinion. They’re definitely not better than the Sixers this season, and many doubt if that team even has a real chance against the Celtics. This would also significantly shorten the championship window. Our young guys are still too young to actually contribute to meaningful wins in the playoffs, and by the time they are ready to do so, the vets would likely be too old.

While we would see the Rockets take a dramatic leap in the standings, it would not be setting the team up for long-term success. We can also trade Jalen for a star player (if his progression continues like it should) anytime, whereas we might not be able to go the opposite way. It takes very specific circumstances for a young player as good as Jalen Green to come anywhere near being traded. Teams usually give players at least a few seasons to get a good sense of who they will become in the future. I guess, in theory, we could trade a bunch of our other young pieces in subsequent trades, but that would put way too much pressure on the players we acquire to win a championship in the very near future. It just doesn’t make sense to go all in right now.

Final Thoughts

Hiring coach Ime Udoka definitely signaled that the Rockets want to start winning games, but I doubt they’ll go to the extent of trading all of their young talent this offseason. They have invested so much in these young players, and I doubt we’ll see them throw that all away and mortgage the future for a 1-2 year chance at potentially winning a title. That concept seems way too radical for Rafael Stone, and that’s why the rumors were quickly disproved. I expect more of a long, drawn-out process. The timeline is currently set up for them to be competitive (as far as winning a championship) in about 3 years. By having home-grown talent, we will have plenty more chances to win it all than with this alternative. I know people want to get back to the playoffs, but making decisions like these simply isn’t the answer. Great things take time.
