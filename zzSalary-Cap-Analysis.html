The Houston Rocket's Salary Cap Situation Heading Into This Off-Season

The NBA salary cap is one of the league’s defining features. Currently, that number is set at $123.6 million. However, future projections suggest it will be raised to $134 million for next season. The Houston Rockets are going to be the team with the most cap space this summer to take advantage of that increased room. That is, of course, if Tilman Fertitta is willing to spend ludicrous amounts of money in an attempt to turn this team around. More on that later. Everything, contract-wise, has been positioned around this crucial off-season. While there aren’t very many top-level talents hitting the open market, it is still important to analyze the exact situation as we prepare for some drastic changes.

All information used can be found here.

What We Know

Houston has a total of $88,279,161 tied up in dead cap space. This is money that counts towards how much cap space a player would’ve taken up if they were still on the roster. A large part of this is John Wall, who was waived twice for a total of $47,345,760. Now, not all of this money was paid to Wall, but that is how much of our cap space he would’ve taken up if he was never bought out. Because of this figure, our total cap money spent currently for this season is $136,458,076. That’s how the Rockets are able to keep in compliance with the minimum cap requirements despite having an active roster that totals just $48,178,915. Next year, with only the contracts that are set in place, that number jumps to $62,181,257, but the dead space goes away.

This puts the Rockets at $61,669,768 in cap space once you factor in all of the cap holds, which, “represent "placeholders" for pending free agents, unsigned draft picks, offer sheets, etc.” (from link above). That figure is with the increased room in the cap. Also, we can’t go over that number when looking to sign new players in free agency; the rules surrounding such a possibility only apply to existing players on the roster. The only possible player we have eligible for an extension is Kenyon Martin Jr. That means, with almost complete certainty, Houston will remain under the luxury tax even if they give him the most amount of money allowed.

Speculation

So, who can the Rockets reasonably sign with that projected $61,669,768 plus a bit more with the cap hold space potentially available? If we take a look at some of the contracts given out last off-season, some insight can be gained. A few I’d like to point out are Jalen Brunson agreeing to a 4-year, $110 million deal, Tyus Jones’ 2-year, $30,000,000, and Lu Dort’s 5-year, $87,500,000 one. These guys are making 27.5, 15, and 17.5 million dollars per year respectively. That means we have space for around 3 guys in a similar tier. I have several articles related to the topic, but in short, a few names that stand out to me are Jerami Grant, Khris Middleton, Caris LeVert, Harrison Barnes, Gary Trent Jr., Myles Turner, and Cameron Johnson. Middleton is obviously the leader of that group so maybe you pair him with one other or choose a total of 3.

The biggest worry I have about this is that Tilman Fertitta could potentially be unwilling to spend more than is required to comply with the minimum of 90% of the cap, equating to a total of $120,600,000. Even so, the Rockets still have to sign somebody with the remaining $48,269,768. That brings it down to 2 of the players mentioned above, but that’s still better than what we have now. Nevertheless, I still have faith that Fertitta isn’t as bad of an owner as most people try to make it seem. He has spent all of this money on players who don’t even play for the team, yet is still widely criticized. Not to mention, the numerous times he has given other organizations money for second round picks. Fertitta was just presented with a plan to rebuild, and I believe he does want to win and will spend a decent amount to do so.

Final Thoughts

Our roster will be so much better once we have a few veterans just to steady the ship. The reason our future draft picks this season aren’t factored into the free agency numbers is because they technically aren’t signed, most times, until after free agency. They still won’t go into the luxury tax, though, because rookie contracts are relatively small in comparison. I also feel like Kevin Porter Jr.’s contract has limited some of our flexibility this summer. In my eyes, that’s a potential trade candidate if he’s not open to a 6th man role. I decided not to include Harden’s return in this analysis because I seriously hope that doesn’t happen. His contract would eat up a good majority of our available space, and he’s just not the right fit for this team (if you want to know why, click here). Anyways, I just can’t wait for the season to be over and watch all of this unfold.
