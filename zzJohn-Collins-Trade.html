Should the Rockets Go After John Collins At the 2023 NBA Trade Deadline

Trade season is beginning to heat up. The deadline is now just over a week away, on February 9th, and it’s that time of year where general managers across the league feel especially pressured to make a move. There are usually 3 categories of teams around trade season: buyers, sellers, and the ones who don’t do anything. The unique part of the Rockets is that they can realistically do all 3. That’s why there’s still very little direction on what might actually happen. On January 27th, the Athletic, a reliable insider NBA publisher, linked the Rockets to forward John Collins, currently on the Atlanta Hawks. Does this even make sense for Houston? The short answer, in my option, is no, but if you want the longer answer, continue reading.

John Collins Player Profile

John Collins is a 6’9”, 235 lb power forward who is currently averaging 13.5 points, 7.7 rebounds, 1.2 assists, 1.2 blocks, and 0.7 steals per game in about 32 minutes of work. The scary part is that his production has been on a steady decline since 2020, even though he’s just 25 years old. This could be due to him constantly being in trade rumors and not being utilized properly in Atlanta, but it’s certainly something worth noting. His shooting percentages inside aren’t terrible as he’s hitting 62% of his shots from 2-point range. However, his 3-ball has been absolutely atrocious at just 26% conversion rate on 3 attempts a game. That’s worse than Russell Westbrook, for comparison.

On the other hand, Collins does have good athleticism, rebounding, and a bit of defensive ability. He also has that hustle and energy factor, which certain guys can bring to an NBA roster. I’m still worried about his shot-creation ability and his injury history, though. He also seems to work best with a traditional big-man alongside him, which doesn’t exactly cater towards Alperen Sengun. He recently signed an extension worth 5 years/$125 million, in which he is in year 2 of. Like I mentioned before, Collins has been in trade rumors for over a year at this point, and it seems like everyday could be his last in Atlanta. I don’t know exactly why this is the case; probably something to do with the fact that Hawks haven’t been very successful as a team since handing out his extension.

Why the Rockets Should Stay Away

There’s obviously a reason Collins hasn’t been moved yet, and that’s because either the asking price is too high or he’s simply not as good anymore. First off, his contract is an overpay. He’s a role player at best, and the Hawks had to offer him that money or risk losing him for nothing. Furthermore, he doesn’t fit our lineup whatsoever. Does he start at the small forward position? Come off the bench? He’d basically just be a slightly better version of Kenyon Martin Jr., who’s only making less than $2 million. That's a big difference money-wise, whereas not so big a difference talent-wise. Martin still may want to leave Houston, but there’s better replacements out there. We’re simply not in a position to overpay for a guy who realistically isn’t going to help us get to the playoffs.

Another thing to mention is that the Rockets have a load of cap space this summer to potentially sign other players who could be cheaper or fit what the team actually needs. I also don’t think Collins is the type of guy to acquire for a veteran presence. He’s simply not been in the NBA long enough to know how to set a standard and hold other guys accountable. The package suggested in the Athletic article is just laughable from our perspective. Why would we give up Eric Gordon, Kenyon Martin Jr., the Nets 2027 pick swap, and our own 2025 first round pick, lottery protected, just for John Collins and a lottery protected first from the Suns in 2026. That makes zero sense for us. The highest we should be willing to go is Martin and 1 first from the Bucks’ one this year, or Nets’ one next year.

Final Thoughts

Trading for John Collins is by no means the right move, especially not at the proposed asking price. Maybe ask me again once Houston can start winning on their own, and needs just one more piece, but for now I don’t see any reason to trade assets away for him. Let’s be honest, Rafael Stone isn’t going to do anything to improve this roster for this season. He wants the team to tank, and making a transaction like this essentially does the opposite. We need to keep the picks and players we have for now unless a trade comes along that’s just too good to pass up. We do not need to rush the rebuild by assembling a roster that will lead to NBA mediocrity. I know the losing is miserable right now, but there’s internal issues with coaching and players that need to be addressed first. We need to hold out on buying until at least this off-season. Doing something like this now is just not the right decision.
