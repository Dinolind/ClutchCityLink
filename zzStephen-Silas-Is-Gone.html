Stephen Silas Is Officially Gone. A Recap of His Tenure + What Happens Next?

As you’ve probably heard, the Houston Rockets organization has officially declined Stephen Silas’ 4th year contract option, making him no longer the head coach of this team. Obviously, that means a new coach is going to be named. Some candidates that have already come up in this discussion are Nick Nurse, Ime Udoka, and Kenny Atkinson. However, before I dive into that deliberation in the future, I wanted to discuss Silas’ time with the organization and what his exit could mean going forward. His tenure has been full of ups and downs, mostly downs if I’m telling the truth, but it still deserves reflection.

Silas’ Tenure

Stephen Silas was hired at a time when the Rockets were still attempting to be actively competitive within the Western Conference. They were coming off a season with James Harden and Russell Westbrook, losing to the Los Angeles Lakers in the second round. However, not all hope was lost because that season was the “bubble” season, and the team was playing surprisingly well before the entire league got shut down. When Silas was hired, he was expected to bring a fresh perspective, and help this team win an NBA championship. Unfortunately, that didn’t happen, as both Harden and Westbrook were traded, and the team unofficially entered a rebuild.

Since then, Silas has been constantly questioned and ridiculed for his decisions made regarding the roster. His rotations were inexplicably bad at times, and it became apparent that he wasn’t the right fit for a young team like the Rockets. Despite this, he was kept around for a total of 3 years. These years will probably go down as the worst stretch of Rockets’ basketball in franchise history. All the blame shouldn’t have been placed solely on Silas, but that’s basically what happened. He became regarded as the worst coach in the NBA by many Rockets fans. That being said, the national media still had his back. This season also coincided with the passing of his father, Paul Silas, making matters even worse. It became clear that his days with the organization were numbered, which leads us to today.

Going Forward

An article has already been released by The Athletic detailing everything from Silas’ tenure. He struggled to establish accountability and wasn't able to form a strong connection with some players, namely Christian Wood and Kevin Porter Jr. The article cites “an overall lack of leadership” as one of the main problems facing Houston. It basically just confirms most of what we knew already. The offense was a mess with no real structure, and defense became optional. If you want to read the full article, click here. One overall theme, though, is that Silas fell short of expectations. He may have been placed in an impossible situation, but didn’t do very well when trying to fix it.

However, with him out, the spotlight should start to shift towards ownership and the front office. Their grand plan needs to be executed properly and in a timely manner. If Rafael Stone fails to live up to expectations, his job could be on the line next. Furthermore, the new coach hired is going to reveal how serious this organization is about turning things around. If they cheap out again and get an unproven rookie, then that’s a massive red flag. We need someone who’s going to instill confidence and identity with our young players, and establish a culture that has previously not existed. So much depends on this summer as to whether or not this team will leap into contention or be stuck in rebuild mode for the next 5+ years. A new phase is upon us, and essentially firing the head coach is a primary signal of that.

Final Thoughts

From everything I’ve heard, Stephen Silas is a great guy but a terrible coach. Although he may have been placed in an impossible scenario, that cannot excuse many of his deficiencies. The locker room had completely turned on him, and everybody knew his time was up. There’s just no way to present this idea of “phase 2” to fans while keeping the same guy everybody has wanted gone for months. I’m just interested in what else comes out from this situation. It seems like the narrative is going to be portrayed as that it’s everybody’s fault but Silas. There are other issues, yes, but that idea is simply not true. The national media is going to label Houston as a place that broke down one of the most respected people in the NBA landscape. Silas is so connected internally, that I fear what could happen. This story isn’t over. There’s going to be more that comes out sooner rather than later.
