Setting Goals For The the Final Few Months of the Rockets’ Season

After tonight, the Rockets will be down to their last 25 games for the 2022-2023 season. With the trade deadline over, this is likely the roster that will finish out the year. As a result, it’s time to set some expectations for the team as the year wraps up. We cannot waste these final few months, especially considering that Houston wants to make a huge leap in the standings by this time next season. Since we’ve already, likely, been eliminated from playoff contention, our focus needs to lie on something besides just winning games. To find out what that is, continue reading.

Figure Out Who to Keep

The Rockets have a plethora of young talent, and are going to have at least 2 picks in this year’s upcoming NBA draft. That means we have to decide who to keep and who to flip for veterans this off-season. Obviously, Jalen Green, Jabari Smith Jr., Alperen Sengun, and whoever is taken with their top selection this year will probably be safe, but what about the others? For example, Josh Christopher, Usman Garuba, Tari Eason, Daishen Nix, Tyty Washington Jr., and Kevin Porter Jr., all could be on the move if they don’t perform well in the remaining games. The bottom line is we just want to make sure the latter group is getting a significant portion of the available minutes, so that we’re prepared to make those tough decisions when the time comes.

Better Record Than Last Year

The Rockets current record sits at 13-43 with 26 games left in the season. Last year they finished 20-62. This means Houston needs to go 8-18 for the rest of the season to show at least some level of improvement compared to last year. That’s totally doable, and I think it would be beneficial for this group to experience some level of progress in what otherwise has been an objectively dreadful season. It could potentially affect their draft pick, but that’s a sacrifice I’m willing to make because the bottom 3 teams have the same chance to get the number 1 pick in the draft anyways.

Limiting Turnovers

One way to ensure the Rockets are able to win more games than last year is to limit how many times they turn the ball over a night. In fact, Houston currently averages the most turnovers per night out of any team in the league at a whopping 17. That’s absolutely atrocious and needs to be decreased if this team wants any chance of improving. I understand there’s going to be a few dumb turnovers a game because the roster is incredibly young, but there are some that are just unacceptable. This just needs to be an area of focus for both the players and coaching staff so that we can fix some of the problems now before it really starts to matter next year.

Improving Defense

Another thing to focus on should be the team’s defense. A lot of defense is just effort, which should not be an issue for a team with so much energy, yet it has been for a majority of the season. It’s been a little better over these past few games, and I want that trend to continue moving forward. The Rockets currently have the 3rd worst defensive rating, just ahead of the Spurs and Pistons, and I would like to see them more towards the middle of the pack by April. Players have had plenty of time to make mistakes and learn from them, so now we need to see improvement. No NBA team has ever made it very far without defense; just look at the Brooklyn Nets.

Final Thoughts

It seems like there’s a new sense of energy and care from these players recently. Stephen Silas has finally been holding guys accountable, and not continuing to accept the same mistakes and lackluster effort we’ve seen on a nightly basis over the course of the season. Now is a perfect time to build up confidence and a reputation that can really help Houston win considerably more games next season. The steps are going to be gradual, yes, but they need to at least start. I expect a new identity to be formed as time progresses, the same identity we were promised at the beginning of this season. Eric Gordon, Garrison Mathews, and Bruno Fernando being gone could be a lot more impactful than we first expected.
