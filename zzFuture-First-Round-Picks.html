Analyzing the Houston Rockets' First Round Draft Picks For the Next Few Years.

The Houston Rockets have accumulated a decent amount of draft capital over these past few years, but where do all of their picks fall currently and what implications could that have moving forward? Obviously, we have our own pick for this NBA draft, which is the reason for undertaking yet another rebuilding season. However, the picks beyond that get a little bit more complicated. There are three main trades impacting the following information: James Harden to the Nets, Russell Westbrook to the Rockets, and Eric Gordon to the Clippers. All 3 deals are going to greatly affect the Rockets over the coming seasons, so it’s an important topic to discuss.

James Harden to Nets

Picks to Houston: 2023 swap with Brooklyn, 2024 pick, 2025 swap with Brooklyn, 2026 pick, 2027 swap with Brooklyn (All unprotected)

Because of this trade, Houston now controls the future of the draft for Brooklyn until 2027. This is especially important considering that the Nets decided to trade both Kevin Durant and Kyrie Irving at the most recent deadline. In my opinion, we should be looking to trade most, if not all, of these future selections. The 2023 swap has no value to us this season anymore, so it’s essentially a goner at this point. Beyond that, though, the rest of the draft assets are likely to be very appealing to other NBA teams. Two picks and two swaps along with one or two young guys should be enough to get an above average, if not near all-star, caliber player. There is no sense in actually drafting with these picks, so be prepared for when something happens over the next year or two.

Russell Westbrook to Rockets

Picks to OKC from Houston: 2024 (protected 1-4), 2025 swap (protected 1-20), 2026 (protected 1-4)

This trade may go down as one of the worst in Rockets’ history. We traded away basically 3 years of draft assets for one shortened season with Russell Westbrook that amounted to a second-round gentleman's sweep by the Los Angeles Lakers. Anyways, because of this, Houston has no incentive to be at the bottom of the league past this season. It also creates some issues with our future asset pool. We’re now in a position to not be able to trade a pick for any draft until 2028 (the extra year because of the Stepien Rule). Usually, as teams emerge from a rebuild, they trade their own draft assets for more veterans, but we simply don’t have that option. Even in a completely new era of basketball, we still have to deal with what the previous administration left behind. It is interesting to note, though, that we will have at least one first round pick in every draft.

Eric Gordon to Clippers

Picks to Houston: Swap Milwaukee 1st 2023 for Clippers 1st (top-6 protected)

Eric Gordon to the Los Angeles Clippers was the biggest move Houston made at the deadline and for good reason. They were able to swap their 2023 Bucks pick, acquired from James Harden and subsequent PJ Tucker trades, with the Clippers one for this season. If the season were to end today, that pick would jump from #30 to #19. That’s a huge gap already, and it could get even bigger if the Clippers continue to lose games. If, for example, they fell down all the way to the 12th seed by going on a losing streak to end the season, that selection suddenly comes into the lottery. It’s an interesting phenomenon to watch. If the Rockets aren’t playing, that doesn’t mean their future isn’t being impacted. Essentially, we’ll have the Clippers first and our own if no further transactions are made. Unless, Los Angeles somehow magically ends up in the top 6, which is incredibly unlikely.

Conclusion

If you’re looking for a more complete breakdown of our draft assets in the future, check out this article on RocketsWire that goes more in depth about every last one until 2029. I have noticed one error, however, which is that our swap with OKC is top-20 protected, not top-10 (per Shams). I think Houston is set up to have success when trading those Brooklyn picks. It has worked out pretty much perfectly for us. However, owing our own picks to the Thunder is very concerning. If the Rockets don’t make drastic changes this off-season to ensure at least a play-in spot, we could be looking at another season of basically a 50/50 chance to even make the losing worth it. That was scary in 2021, and it will be even scarier next time around. I’m just glad we don’t have to worry about it for at least a little while. I’m more intrigued by how much it will change the moves made this summer. Prepare to see them be very aggressive when putting together a competent roster heading into next season. A sense of urgency is looming.
