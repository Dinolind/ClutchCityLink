Assesing How Important It Really Is to Build a Winning Culture For the Houston Rockets

A lot of the discourse among fans of the Houston Rockets has centered around one topic: Should the team be focused on winning games? It seems there are two sides to this issue. On one side you have people fully embracing the tank arguing that it is the only proper way to move forward; on the other you have a group who is sick of watching the team lose repeatedly with no end in sight. With yet another loss against the Charlotte Hornets on January 18th, the losing-streak now totals 12 in a row and 17 of their last 18. Now feels like a turning point in the season and I wanted to take a chance to explore the different possibilities and assess how much building a winning culture actually matters in the long run.

Benefits of Losing

The most obvious perk of continuing to lose games is the chance at Victor Wembenyama or Scoot Henderson in this year’s upcoming NBA draft. Both players look to be generational prospects are a big part of why there are so many teams have sold some of their talent in preparation for this season. This decision also means that the Rockets’ future draft assets are perceived to hold more value if the team continues to play at this level. It does make sense out of necessity, too, because it does no good to stay in the middle of the pack when it comes to the NBA. Ideally, you want to be first, i.e. winning the championship, or dead last for the highest lottery odds. It may make sense for the Rockets to choose the latter because they don’t have the players or trade tokens to make this roster capable of winning it all.

Furthermore, it is not smart in any way to rush the completion of this rebuild before the pieces are in place to have made the rebuild worth it. A majority of the players are either in their rookie or sophomore seasons, and have had the time needed to develop into their potential just yet. If we cash in on these promising guys now, we may regret it when they do start to progress somewhere else. There’s also less pressure on said players to progress because the expectation is to lose games anyways. The final point I want to make is that there’s a lot more flexibility when dealing with unproven talent as opposed to veterans. Generally, rookie contracts are a way to keep costs low, increasing salary cap space, and there’s always the option to turn them into more established guys. It’s much harder to go in the opposite direction (older role players to picks / younger prospects).

Benefits of Winning

Winning has been defined more as a culture than anything, and it’s impossible to build that culture overnight. The Clippers are a perfect example of a team who has everything, in theory, required for success, yet has been unable to put it together in a solid playoff run. The Nets have had the same problem. Star players only can get you so far, it takes much more than that for a team to be great. If we continue down the path of simply giving up, it may be extremely difficult to erase those bad habits and the reputation built when the time comes to compete. Teaching accountability and defining structure is not an easy process for any organization, and fully attempting to win games now could prevent underlying issues down the line. It’s just something to think about.

Additionally, the coaching staff will continue to lose respect if positive changes aren’t made, and the damage caused now could end up being so irreversible that we have to overhaul the entire roster again, which is exactly what we should be trying to avoid at all costs. Besides, it’s not like rebuilds have a very good track record anyways. The 76ers, Suns, Nets, and Bulls all went with the complete reset option recently, similar to what the Rockets are doing now, and none of them have been crowned the best team since. There are very few success stories compared to a ton of failures. Normally it takes luck in drafting and developing an all-time great before pairing that person with the right players around them. The past two champions, Bucks and Warriors, both implemented this strategy.

Final Thoughts

Houston is not going to magically start winning games this season, so don’t get your hopes up. The front office has clearly chosen a direction and it’s simply too late to change their minds now. I will be very disappointed if this is not the last year of being unusually bad. We cannot keep up the worst team in the league streak any longer; otherwise there may be some terrible consequences. Unfortunately, I don’t think it is perceived this way by the people in charge. Hopefully they do have a plan to make dramatic changes this summer, but if so, they aren’t utilizing this season effectively to see what changes actually need to be made. At the end of the day, everything could come back to the franchise being a business. As long as the payroll is cheap and the fans are not rioting, the higher ups could be happy with the lackluster play on a game-to-game basis. Building a competent roster is not cheap, and that could be another big reason why it isn’t happening.
