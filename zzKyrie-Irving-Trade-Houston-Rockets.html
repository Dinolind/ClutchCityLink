How the Kyrie Irving Dallas Mavericks Trade Impacts the Houston Rockets

Kyrie Irving was traded from the Brooklyn Nets to the Dallas Mavericks on February 5th, 2023 ahead of the trade deadline this Thursday. You probably already know the details by now, but in case you don’t, they are as follows:

Mavericks Received: Kyrie Irving, Markieff Morris

Nets Received: Spencer Dinwiddie, Dorian Finney-Smith, 2029 first-round pick, 2027 second-round pick, 2029 second-round pick (all picks unprotected via Dallas)

So, how does this all impact the Rockets? Obviously, a star player being traded is one of the things fans live for, unless that player is a part of the team they support. However, for Houston, the effects go a lot deeper than just that. There’s still a lot we don’t know, as the trade literally just happened a few hours ago, at the time of writing, and there are still a lot of questions yet to be answered, especially for the Nets. For the Mavericks, the trade was pretty simple. Now they have a star to pair alongside Luka Doncic, and hope to make a deep run in the playoffs this year. Again, we’ll see what actually materializes over the coming months, but everybody is going to be watching closely and waiting for the future to reveal itself.

Current Situation

The main reason the Rockets are so connected to this trade without actually being included in it is the fact that they own the rights to Brooklyn’s first round picks through 2027, as a result of the James Harden trade back in 2021. To be clear, Houston has the Nets’ first round pick outright in 2024 and 2026, and the ability to swap picks with them in 2023, 2025, and 2027. The value of those picks is consequently dependent on the future in Brooklyn, and right now their value is at an all-time high. This means the Rockets may have the trade pieces to turn this roster into a contender over the coming years, without having to give up any valuable players already on the team. The package for Harden, that many fans have called a terrible return, may actually be beginning to show its true value.

Houston is going to exit a rebuilding phase with some of the most sought-after draft capital within the NBA, and it’s all thanks to Rafael Stone. Now, I’m not going to give him all the credit because no one could’ve seen a team with 3 superstars imploding over the course of a year, but it was a risk worth taking, and it’s finally starting to pay off. Ben Simmons, the main piece in the Nets’ return for Harden, is likely never going to be the same player again. As a result, his 5-year/$177,243,360 contract is viewed by many as the worst in the league. Brooklyn is going to have no cap space this summer, no future draft capital, and likely a star player who wants out. Houston, on the other hand, has plenty of cap space this summer, all the Nets’ draft capital, and a ton of young talent. Not to mention, a likely top-5 pick in an absolutely loaded draft after this season.

Future Possibilities

Kevin Durant really determines the future in this scenario. If he’s unhappy with essentially his best friend leaving the organization in exchange for a bunch of role players, we may see his trade request from this past summer reinstated. There is a scenario, in my mind, where the Rockets are the ones who actually have the assets to acquire him if they wanted to. First off, all of Brooklyn’s picks are a nice start, since it’s really difficult to complete a rebuild without your own draft capital. Then there’s one of Jalen Green, Jabari Smith Jr., or our pick from this year’s draft, which could help jumpstart their new roster with, at least, presumably a future all-star. Throw in Eric Gordon and some other guys for salary filler as well as one of Garuba, Christopher, or Washington and I think that’s a package nobody else can beat.

The next step after that trade would be to sign James Harden this off-season, and then the Rockets would be right back in contention with 2 stars and a solid supporting cast. The problem is that that roster is likely not going to win a championship. Harden and KD couldn’t win together in OKC and once again failed in Brooklyn; should we really be throwing away our rebuild for something that has a proven track-record of not panning out as expected? I don’t think so. Durant’s price is going to be way too high for me to even really consider it, and Houston is certainly not ready for that kind of jump or responsibility. The more likely situation is that they become a third team in the deal for Durant. The Nets send KD to team A, team A sends future picks and role players to the Rockets, and the Rockets send different role players and the Nets’ picks to Brooklyn. That’s just a rough framework, but hopefully you get the idea.

Final Thoughts

The truth is, no one really knows, and we likely won’t know until Thursday or perhaps this summer. Kyrie Irving has just shaken up what otherwise would’ve been a pretty boring deadline, per many sources across the league, and we are still yet to see most of the effects. The Nets did not give away their whole future just to be a .500 team for the next few years with role players around Kevin Durant, and Kevin Durant did not leave the Warriors to do that either. He wants to win without being a part of arguably the greatest team of all time, and that likely won’t happen in his current city. The bottom line is that Rockets fans should be overjoyed about the downfall of Brooklyn's big three in such a short time. No one knows what's next, but with trade season ending in just a few days, keep your eyes and ears on high alert.
